nanoDanya: teaching a language model to play chess

An experiment in how far a pure language model can go in chess, and a reminder that cleaner inference wrappers should not be mistaken for deeper learned capability.

By Sparshith · April 8, 2026

nanoDanya is an experiment in how far a pure language model can go in playing chess. The interesting question is not whether the wrapper can be forced into clean games. It is how much chess the model itself can absorb: legal continuation, tactics, strategic play, and clean game termination from next-token prediction alone.

That constraint matters because it changes what counts as progress. Runtime tricks like legality masks or defensive inference heuristics may keep the system running, but they are scaffolding, not the goal.

The real work is in the data, tokenization, training objective, and the model's learned behavior. Those are the levers that can make the model more robust in a way that actually transfers.

I like projects like this because they force the question into the open: what does the model actually know, and what are we just patching around at inference time?
